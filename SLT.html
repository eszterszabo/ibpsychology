Social Learning Theory


The Social Learning Theory (SLT) is a concept proposed by Bandura, which states that learning is a cognitive process, but it takes place in a social context. He believed that it takes place through experience, observations or directly received instructions.
Schemas are also responsible for forming "scripts", which will influence the way we expect events to go and people to behave. Many schemas are based on stereotypes.
This theory works based on the idea that humans are information processors, meaning that when receiving information, we integrate it into our memory by merging it with information that we already know.


RESEARCH:

Bandura et al , 1961: The Bobo doll experiment - The aim of this study was to investigate how social factors influence cognition and memory.
In this experiment, children were divided into 2 groups. Group 1 saw adults playing nicely with the Bobo doll, whereas participants in Group 2 witnessed adults being violent with the Bobo doll, kicking and punching it. Following this, each child was put into a room with the Bobo doll.
The results showed that children who observed the violently behaving adults were also more likely to punch and kick the Bobo doll. This shows that children learn through observation, providing strong evidence for the SLT.
