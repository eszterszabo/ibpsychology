Culture and Cultural Norms


Models of cognition refer to theories about how our memory works. Two of such models are the Multi-store model (MSL) and the Working memory model (WMM). These models explain two different way in which memory formation and retrieval could be explained, although it is important to know that whilst both of these models have evidence to support them, neither of them can be scientifically proven.
The IB requires psychology students to know TWO Models of cognition.


Culture and cultural norms

As distinguished by Triadis in 1980, there are two main elements to culture: Objective aspects, such as the clothes they wera and the foods they eat and the buildings they live in, and Subjective aspects, such as their beliefs, religion and shared values.
Cultural norms, on the other hand, refer to ways of behaviour that are considered to be normal, typical or average in a specific culture.
One example of this could be that in the Islamic culture women wearing a hijab is considered to be the norm, and it is seen unsatisfactory for women not to cover themselves up, while in other cultures is is common for women to wear much more revealing clothes.



Emic and Etic analysis of research on culture

Emic and etic refers to the way that research on a specific culture can be applied to other cultures.
Emic behaviours are seen as non-universally applicable, as people who live in different cultures react to research differently.
One example of this is seen in bed-sharing between parents and their infants. In Western cultures only 5% of infants share their parents´ beds, while in Pacific countries, according to some surveys, approximately 80% of infants share beds with their parents.
Etic refers to the view that research can be universally applied, as people around the world all behave in similar manners. It is said that more complex behaviours are less likely to be emic, while simple, "core" behaviours with biological origins, such as sleep cycles are more likely to be etic.
